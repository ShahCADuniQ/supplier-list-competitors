Housing — Overview

What belongs in the Housing group?

The housing is the mechanical shell that contains, protects, and structurally supports every other subsystem of a luminaire. Across every Lightbase product — linear, recessed, pendant, track, outdoor facade, in-ground, bollard, downlight, or strip — the housing family comprises the same four component classes:

Structural

  • Profile / body — extruded, die-cast, machined, or formed enclosure
  • Covers & lids — removable service faces
  • Internal chassis — driver tray, optic carrier, reflector

Functional

  • Sealing surfaces — gasket grooves, O-ring faces, tongue-in-groove closures
  • Optical window — tempered glass, PMMA, PC, or silicone
  • Cable / gland bosses — entry points for wiring and strain relief
Rule of thumb Every housing part should structure, seal, or transmit light. Heat dissipation is handled in the LED Source group because the thermal path is driven by the LED package, not the enclosure geometry. Mounting and installation hardware lives in Mounting Attachment. Anything that adds a user-visible feature lives in Accessories.

Design questions to answer before drawing the housing

  • Mounting method — surface, recessed, pendant, wall, track, pole, ground, column, cove, custom? This drives wall thickness, flange geometry, and cable entry.
  • Environment — indoor dry, damp, wet, outdoor, marine, hazardous? This drives IP rating, gasket choice, and material selection.
  • Service access — how is the driver replaced? Is the fixture tool-access only, or can a lift crew open it with gloves?
  • Maintenance strategy — is the driver in a remote junction box or integrated in the housing? Integrated drivers must withstand the worst-case internal temperature.
  • Weight — calculate total assembled mass. For ceiling-mounted product, confirm the ceiling or structure supports at least 4× that weight (CSA/UL safety factor).

Profiles & Extrusion Design

Profile Design Philosophy

A profile is the single most design-intensive part of any linear or extruded luminaire. It has to act as structure, heat path, PCB carrier, optical housing, and sealing channel simultaneously — all while being extrudable in one shot through a single die. Good profile design is where mechanical, thermal, and optical engineering meet.

Profile Cross-Section Features

  • PCB channel — nominal PCB width + 0.3 mm clearance per side, slight lead-in chamfer to ease insertion
  • Optic pocket — 0.2 mm clearance around lens base for snap-fit, adhesive, or press-fit
  • Fin array — on the side opposite the LED, never on the same face (radiation blocks light output)
  • Gasket groove — trapezoidal, 10–20% wider at the opening than the gasket's uncompressed thickness
  • Screw bosses — 2.5 mm wall, 4.2 mm pilot ID for M3 self-tapping screws
  • Cable channel — a dedicated internal cable run separated from the LED cavity to keep wires out of the optical path
  • Accessory slot — a secondary T-slot or dovetail rib so the same profile can take endcaps, brackets, suspension cables, or trims from one family of accessories
Common extrusion mistake Designing fins that can't be released from the die — fin aspect ratio over 8:1 (height:gap) causes tongue collapse during extrusion. Keep fin aspect ratio ≤ 6:1 for reliable output, ≤ 10:1 only with die-maker sign-off.

Profile family archetypes

Extrusion design rules (independent of alloy)

  • Keep wall thickness uniform. Thick:thin ratio ≤ 3:1 in the same cross-section — unbalanced walls cause die-line distortion and twisting during quenching.
  • Avoid thin tongues. Any tongue (an internal feature with walls on three sides) must have a thickness ≥ 1/3 the parent wall.
  • Radius everything. Inside radius ≥ 0.5 mm, outside radius ≥ 0.3 mm. Sharp corners fail at the die.
  • Hollow sections need a porthole die. Add ~20% tooling cost and ~2 week lead time.
  • Straightness target: ±1.5 mm/m straight out of the press, ±0.5 mm/m after stretching.
  • Max length: most presses handle 6 m, some facade specialists 7.5 m. Design around 3 m transport length if you don't know the logistics yet.

Aluminum 6063 — Extrusion

Why 6063 for extruded profiles?

AL 6063 is the standard alloy for all Lightbase extruded profiles. It has the lowest flow resistance of the 6xxx series, which means thinner walls, finer fins, and tighter tolerances than 6061 or 6082 can deliver through a die.

Extrusion Design Rules

  • Wall thickness: 1.5mm standard, 1.2mm absolute minimum
  • Wall variation: thicker:thinner ratio ≤ 3:1 in the same cross-section
  • Tongue ratio: fin height ÷ gap ≤ 6:1 (8:1 requires die-maker sign-off)
  • Hollow sections: need a porthole die — adds ~20% tooling cost
  • Corners: inside radius ≥ 0.5mm, outside radius ≥ 0.3mm
  • Screw bosses: 2.5mm wall, 4.2mm ID pilot for M3 self-tap
  • Circumscribing circle: 300–400mm max depending on press tonnage
  • Straightness: ±1.5 mm/m (without post-stretching)
60° Rule for screw holes in extrusions Any screw pocket must have walls that fall within a 60° cone from the centerline, otherwise the die cannot support the tongue and it will collapse under pressure.

Finishing on 6063

  • Powder coat — RAL 7012 grey is Lightbase standard; 60–80 µm thickness
  • Anodizing — Type II (sulfuric) for architectural, Type III (hardcoat) for high-wear
  • Chromate conversion — only as a primer below powder, never as a final finish

Aluminum 6061 — Machining

Why 6061 for machined parts?

AL 6061-T6 is ~40% stronger than 6063 and machines with a much better finish. Use it for any CNC-cut end cap, mounting plate, or bracket that carries load or needs thread engagement deeper than 2×D.

Machining Design Rules

  • Internal corners: must have a radius — minimum 0.5× tool diameter
  • Minimum wall (pocketed): 0.8mm
  • Maximum pocket depth: 4× cavity width (deeper requires special tooling)
  • Thread depth: 1.5× to 2× diameter for aluminum (e.g., M4 → 6–8mm)
  • Through-holes: drill depth ≤ 10× diameter without peck
  • Chamfers: always specify C0.5 minimum on external edges (deburring)
  • Tolerances: ±0.1mm is standard; ±0.05mm costs 2× and requires inspection
Thin-wall trap 6061 work-hardens. Avoid pockets where the final wall drops below 1mm — chatter will destroy the surface finish and the part will distort on release from the vise.

Where we use 6061

  • End caps (LHS threaded / RHS through-hole)
  • Custom mounting brackets with load paths
  • Heat sink bases when fin geometry is CNC-milled
  • Driver enclosure covers that need tapped holes for conduit fittings

Aluminum ADC12 — Die Casting

Why ADC12 for cast parts?

ADC12 (JIS) / A383 (ASTM) is the default aluminum die-casting alloy for lighting housings. High silicon content (≈10.5%) gives it excellent flow into thin sections, low shrinkage, and good thermal conductivity for a cast alloy.

Die Casting Design Rules

  • Wall thickness: 2.0–2.5mm is the sweet spot. <1.5mm risks misruns, >4mm risks porosity
  • Uniformity: wall variation ≤ 2:1 across the part (thermal balance during solidification)
  • Draft angle: 1.5° minimum on side walls, 3° on deep pockets
  • Ribs: thickness = 0.6× parent wall; height ≤ 5× rib thickness
  • Fillets: all internal corners R ≥ 0.5mm (stress risers + flow aids)
  • Parting line: always design a visible parting line — never a "floating" one
  • Ejector pin marks: expect 0.1–0.3mm proud; hide on non-cosmetic faces
  • Holes: cored holes only if length ≤ 4× diameter, else drill post-cast
Porosity budget ADC12 typically carries 1–3% porosity by volume. If a part must be pressure-tight or weldable, specify squeeze-casting or switch to A356 permanent-mold (at 2× cost).

Where we use ADC12

  • Large outdoor heat sinks with complex fin geometry
  • Integrated end caps with mounting features (replaces 2-part 6061 assemblies)
  • IP67 junction boxes and driver housings
  • Facade luminaires where shape freedom > extrusion capability

  • Wall thickness: 1.5–3.0 mm for PC, 2.0–3.0 mm for PMMA
  • Draft angle: 1° minimum, 2° for textured surfaces
  • Rib thickness: 50–60% of adjacent wall to avoid sink marks
  • Snap fit strain: ≤ 2% for PC, ≤ 1% for PMMA (it cracks)

  • Radius: internal R ≥ 0.5× wall; external R ≥ wall + 0.5×
Never snap-fit PMMA Acrylic has almost no plastic deformation — any designed-in flex becomes a crack. Use mechanical fasteners or bonding instead.

AAMA coating hierarchy For outdoor luminaires the coating standard drives the warranty: AAMA 2603 = 1-year, 2604 = 5-year, 2605 = 10+ year. Salt-spray hours required scale from 1,000 (2603) to 3,000+ (2605).

Finish process rules

  • Surface prep is 80% of adhesion. Degrease → etch → chromate (or equivalent) → rinse → bake → coat. Skip any step and the finish flakes in field.
  • Mask threaded holes, ground paths, and optical faces before powder or anodize. Powder in an M4 tap hole means a broken tap on the assembly line.
  • Rack witness marks are unavoidable on anodize and powder. Always place them in non-visible areas — usually inside a gasket groove or behind a cover.
  • Color consistency. Different anodize tanks and powder batches drift. For multi-part assemblies specify the same batch on the PO and require ΔE ≤ 1.5 between mating parts.
  • Dissimilar-metal contact. Under powder, always separate aluminum from steel with an isolator (nylon washer, epoxy primer) to prevent galvanic corrosion in humid environments.

Material Compatibility & Outgassing

Why this section exists

This is a critical but frequently overlooked failure mode in sealed LED luminaires. Volatile compounds from the wrong gasket, adhesive, flux residue, or even a cardboard shipping insert can migrate into the LED cavity, deposit on the silicone encapsulant and phosphor, and irreversibly darken the light output — sometimes within weeks.

Never use sulfur-vulcanized rubber gaskets, O-rings, or thermal pads near LEDs. Sulfur migrates through silicone encapsulant and reacts with the silver reflector inside the LED, causing permanent dark spots and total output collapse. Use silicone or peroxide-cured EPDM only. This rule applies to every product, indoor or outdoor.

Material compatibility checklist

  • No sulfur-containing materials. Eliminate vulcanized rubber, certain thermal pads, corrugated cardboard inside the LED compartment.
  • No high-VOC materials in sealed cavities. Outgassing deposits on LED and lens surfaces reduce output and CCT consistency.
  • Adhesives. Verify LED-compatible. Avoid silicone-based RTV that outgasses acetic acid. Use LED-safe neutral-cure silicone or UV-cure acrylics.
  • Solder flux. Clean after reflow. Flux residue can outgas in sealed enclosures at temperature.
  • Potting compound. If potting electronics in the LED compartment, use a low-outgassing formulation (NASA ASTM E595 < 1.0% TML).
  • Conformal coating. Verify compatibility with LED encapsulant; avoid silicones and amine-cured epoxies near optical surfaces.
  • Packaging. Remove all cardboard, foam, paper, and desiccant from the LED compartment before final seal.
  • Cleaning solvents. IPA only. Never acetone (cracks PC/PMMA) or MEK (attacks anodize and powder).
Industry reference Luminus APN-002816, Cree LED-chemical compatibility, Samsung AN-T011, and Osram chemical compatibility guidelines all test materials in the LED optical cavity for sulfur content and VOC outgassing. A 1,000 hr sealed-cavity test at 85°C / 85% RH on a pre-production prototype is cheap insurance compared to a field recall.

Thermal expansion rules

Linear LED fixtures and long extruded profiles move measurably when they heat up. Forget to design for that movement and the housing will distort, the gasket will lose compression, or the lens will bow.

  • Aluminum: α ≈ 23 µm/m/°C. A 1.2 m fixture with 50°C rise grows 1.4 mm.
  • Steel: α ≈ 12 µm/m/°C — roughly half of aluminum.
  • PMMA / PC: α ≈ 70–85 µm/m/°C — 3× aluminum. Lens covers need slotted mounts.
  • Tempered glass: α ≈ 9 µm/m/°C — almost immovable.
Linear run rule For continuous runs > 2 m, always design slip joints between sections. One end fixed, the other free to grow — otherwise the profile will bow or tear its mounting hardware free at full operating temperature.

Stack-up analysis

Before any design freeze, run a worst-case tolerance stack-up on every interface where parts mate:

  • Optic alignment to LED pad (critical for beam consistency — target < ±0.10 mm)
  • Lens cover to gasket groove (must still compress 15–25% at worst-case assembly)
  • PCB slot to profile channel (prevents PCB bow and thermal de-coupling)
  • Fastener pattern to mating part (don't let a 4-hole pattern jam on the last screw)
GD&T on drawings Apply position, flatness, and parallelism callouts on any critical feature — gasket grooves, PCB seating surfaces, optic seating features, and sealing faces. Dimensional ± only is not enough.

Heat Dissipation — Fundamentals

The thermal path

Every watt of electricity entering a luminaire comes out either as light or as heat. Modern LED packages are 30–45% efficient — meaning the other 55–70% must be moved from the junction to ambient air, through a chain of thermal resistances:

Junction → Solder joint → PCB → Thermal interface → Heat sink base → Fins → Air

Each link adds resistance (°C/W). The total resistance, times the dissipated power, equals the temperature rise above ambient. The goal: keep the LED junction below 85°C for lifetime > 50,000 hours.

Key thermal resistances (typical values)

LinkValueNotes
Rj-s (junction to solder)3 – 8 °C/WFrom LED datasheet
Rs-b (solder to PCB)0.5 – 2 °C/WDepends on copper fill, thermal vias
Rb-h (PCB to heat sink)1 – 4 °C/WTIM: silicone grease or pad
Rh-a (heat sink to ambient)2 – 20 °C/WThis is what fin design controls
Where fin design matters Only the last link (heat sink to air) is a design variable once you've picked your LED. Fin surface area, geometry, spacing, and orientation all live here.

Natural vs forced convection

Natural convection (passive)

  • No moving parts, no noise, zero maintenance
  • Typical h ≈ 5–10 W/m²·K
  • Vertical fin orientation is 30–40% more efficient than horizontal
  • Works for < 150 W per luminaire

Forced convection (fan)

  • Typical h ≈ 25–100 W/m²·K
  • 3–5× more heat rejected from the same footprint
  • Fans fail before LEDs — warranty risk
  • Only justified above ~200 W

Thermal Budget and Junction Temperature

The budget chain

A thermal budget is a one-line calculation that has to balance for every LED luminaire, regardless of size, package, or use case:

Tj = Ta + Pd × (Rθj-c + Rθc-b + Rθb-h + Rθh-a)

Where Tj is the LED junction temperature, Ta is the worst-case ambient, Pd is the dissipated power (not electrical input — only the fraction that becomes heat), and the Rθ terms are the thermal resistances along the path.

Target junction temperatures (independent of LED brand)

GoalTarget TjNotes
L90 at 100,000 hr≤ 85 °CArchitectural, indoor default
L80 at 60,000 hr≤ 95 °CGeneral commercial
L70 at 50,000 hr≤ 105 °CMinimum ENERGY STAR compliance
Stressed / short life≤ 120 °CEmergency lights, short duty cycle
Driver matters too Electrolytic capacitors in LED drivers lose ~50% of their rated life for every 10°C above the driver's Tc spec. A 70°C-rated driver running at 80°C internal ambient is the #1 root cause of "LED fixture" failures — even though the LED itself is fine.

Typical resistances in the path

LinkValueDesign lever
Rθj-c (junction → case)1 – 10 °C/WFixed — pick the LED part number
Rθc-b (case → PCB)0.5 – 3 °C/WSolder quality, thermal vias, pad pattern
Rθb-h (PCB → heat sink)0.5 – 4 °C/WTIM choice, bolt torque, flatness

Reserve design margin Always design for at least 10°C of margin below the target Tj. This covers manufacturing variation in thermal interface materials, mounting torque variation, and ambient extremes that weren't in the spec.

Methodology & formulas used

Step 1 — Heat to dissipate. Not all electrical power becomes heat; a fraction becomes light. The remainder must be removed from the junction:

Pheat = Pelectrical × (1 − ηLED)

Step 2 — Allowable sink-to-air resistance. Given a target junction temperature, ambient temperature, and the fixed junction-to-case resistance from the LED datasheet:

Rθs-a,max = (Tj − Tamb) / Pheat − Rθj-c

Step 3 — Required fin surface area. Using Newton's law of cooling with an effective convection coefficient h (5 W/m²·K for horizontal natural, 7 W/m²·K for vertical natural, 25 W/m²·K for forced), and a temperature delta of (Tj − Tamb):

Arequired = Pheat / (h × ΔT)

Step 4 — Fin count. Each fin contributes 2 × L × H of surface area. Ideal fin spacing for natural convection is 5–8 mm; closer fins start to choke the boundary layer.

Nfins = ⌈ Arequired / (2 × Lprofile × Hfin) ⌉

Step 5 — Estimated junction temperature. Back-calculated from the actual delivered fin area to show real thermal margin:

Tj,est = Tamb + Pheat × (Rθj-c + 1/(h × Aactual))

Assumptions & limits:

  • Fin efficiency assumed ≥ 0.9 (valid when material k > 150 W/m·K and fin aspect ratio < 6)
  • Radiation is ignored at sink temperatures < 70°C — it adds roughly 15% extra dissipation if surfaces are anodized black
  • Steady-state only; transient thermal response is not modeled
  • Inter-fin spacing effects are approximated, not solved via CFD

For critical designs, always validate with thermocouple measurement on a prototype under worst-case ambient conditions.

Core Design›Mounting Attachment›Mounting Overview

Mounting & Installation Hardware — Overview

What lives in this group?

Mounting Attachment covers every component whose job is to physically hold the luminaire in place and transfer its weight to the host structure. This is a deliberate separation from Accessories (end caps, gaskets, glass) and from the Housing body: mounting hardware is what changes when the same product is offered in surface, pendant, recessed, or track variants.

What belongs here

  • Aircraft cables and pendant rods
  • Brackets, yokes, trunnions
  • Canopies and J-box covers
  • Plastic clips and spring clips
  • Track adapters and magnetic bases
  • Safety cables (always required for suspended)

What does NOT belong

  • End caps — that's Accessories
  • Fasteners — that's Accessories
  • Gaskets and O-rings — that's Accessories
  • Cable glands for wiring — that's Accessories
  • Structural extrusion of the body — that's Housing

Universal mounting rules

  • Safety factor. 4× minimum for all suspension hardware per CSA and UL. A fixture weighing 2 kg needs hardware rated for 8 kg.
  • Redundant suspension. Any fixture above 1.5 m off the floor, or above people, needs an independent safety cable separate from the primary suspension.
  • Maintenance access. Design for one-person installation. If the fixture has to be aligned, leveled, and terminated simultaneously, the install team will drop it.
  • Wire before mount vs after. Decide the installation sequence in the design phase — is the electrician wiring at ground then lifting, or wiring overhead? This affects the cable entry direction and gland placement.
  • Adjustability. Call out the tilt, rotation, and aiming range explicitly, and provide a locking mechanism that survives repeated torquing.
  • Tool-free service. Wherever possible, hardware should allow reopening for service without special tools — a flathead or Phillips only.

Aircraft Cables & Suspension Hardware

Cable selection

Aircraft cable (stranded stainless steel wire rope) is the default suspension medium for pendant linear, pendant round, and feature fixtures. It carries the load, delivers power when the cable itself is the conductor, and gives the installer fine height adjustment via a gripper.

Stainless grade Use SS304 (A2) for indoor dry and damp locations. Use SS316 (A4) for outdoor, coastal, pool, and food-processing environments — A2 pits in salt spray within 18 months.

Gripper and termination

  • Self-gripping canopy grippers — spring-loaded release button for infinite height adjustment; the most common termination for pendant product.
  • Swaged ball ends — for canopies with a captive ball socket. Cable length is fixed; no field adjustment.
  • Loop and crimp sleeve — for eye-bolt suspension from structure. Verify load rating on the crimp.
  • Live cable (power-carrying). Where the cable doubles as the conductor, use a dedicated twin-core safety-cable system with UL and CSA listing. Never splice power through a self-gripping canopy.

Design rules

  • Two cables minimum per linear fixture, spaced at 20–25% from each end (classic mechanical beam support positioning).
  • Safety cable — a third, slack cable rated for the full fixture weight, anchored independently of the power/support path.
  • Aim angle. Cables must run within ±2° of vertical under the installed load — angled cables induce side force on the canopy and can unscrew it over time.
  • Minimum clearance from the canopy to the fixture: 100 mm, so the installer has a tool path.
Core Design›Mounting Attachment›Brackets & Yokes

Brackets, Yokes & Trunnions

Bracket families

  • Surface brackets — low-profile sheet metal or extrusion pieces that bolt the fixture directly to a wall, ceiling, or soffit. Typical material: CRS powder-coated or SS304. Best for indoor dry locations.
  • Wall-mount with tilt — stamped SS304 or formed aluminum plate with a slotted arc for ±15°–±45° tilt adjustment. Used on wall washers, wall grazers, and display lighting.
  • Yokes (two-arm brackets) — U-shaped brackets supporting a round or square fixture on trunnion pivots. Standard for outdoor floods, facade projectors, and sport lighting.
  • Recessed flange / encastre brackets — short extrusion pieces that lock into the backside of a recessed profile and provide a bearing surface against ceiling drywall or a T-bar.
  • Pole and arm brackets — for pole-top and wall-arm outdoor product. Always design for wind load per ASCE 7 / NBC 2020 and include a safety restraint.

Bracket design rules

  • Load path. The load must pass through the bolted fasteners into solid metal — not through a bent tab or a single stitch weld.
  • Aiming lock. Any adjustable bracket needs a positive lock (serrated washer, bolted clamp, or indexed tooth plate). Friction alone drifts under thermal cycling.
  • Thermal isolation. For LED fixtures where the bracket touches a hot housing, spec a thermal break so the bracket temperature stays below 70°C at the mounting surface — drywall code limit.
  • Dissimilar metals. Aluminum bracket on steel structure must have a nylon washer or epoxy-primed contact face to prevent galvanic corrosion.
  • Seismic restraint where applicable — especially for suspended grid installations in seismic zones. Reference the local code and add a seismic cable or aircraft-cable restraint.

Magnetic mounting

Magnetic mounting is common for modular low-voltage track systems (48 V DC rails) where fixtures snap into a ferromagnetic channel. The magnet holds the fixture; contact pins deliver power.

Core Design›Accessories›End Caps

End Caps

What end caps do

End caps are the closures at each end of a linear or tubular luminaire. They seal the profile ends, provide an anchor point for the sealing gasket, route cable exits, and on some products carry the mounting features. Across the whole product catalog, end caps fall into three families:

  • Machined aluminum (6061-T6 CNC) — load-bearing, high-precision, used on premium linears and outdoor product. Easy to thread, easy to gasket.
  • Die-cast aluminum (ADC12) — high-volume (> 5,000 units/year), complex shapes, integrated mounting features. Lower unit cost but 5–8 week tooling lead time.
  • Injection-molded plastic (PC, PC/ABS) — low-power indoor product only. Cheap, light, but limited in clamp force and temperature range.

End cap design rules

  • Asymmetric by convention. One end threaded, the other through-hole. This prevents assembly line mistakes and ensures fastener direction is consistent across a product family.
  • Gasket contact face — flatness ≤ 0.1 mm across the sealing perimeter. Out-of-flat end caps are the #1 cause of IP failures in linear product.
  • Cable pass-through — always chamfered and deburred; add an internal strain-relief grommet and route the cable clear of any gasket crush zone.
  • Thermal coupling — if the end cap is part of the heat path, spec a TIM pad between cap and profile. Otherwise design it as a pure seal component.
  • Finish match. Powder-coat the cap in the same batch as the profile to keep ΔE ≤ 1.5. Anodized caps and powder-coated profiles never match — pick one and commit.
  • Captive fasteners — loose screws get lost on a ladder. Use captive shoulder screws or PEM inserts that hold the fastener in the cap.

  • Silicone — the default for LED fixtures. Peroxide-cured, 40–70 Shore A. Wide temp range (−50°C to +200°C), LED-safe.

Selection sequence

Binning & Color Consistency

What you're binning

At the end of an LED production line every die is measured for flux, forward voltage, and chromaticity coordinates on the CIE 1931 diagram. LEDs are then sorted into bins. A fixture that draws from multiple bins will show visible mismatches — especially on linear runs, grazing light, and wall-wash fields where colour differences become obvious side-by-side.

Ellipse / SDCM targets

A MacAdam ellipse (SDCM — Standard Deviation of Colour Matching) describes how far two chromaticity points can drift before a trained observer can tell them apart. Bigger number = looser bin = more variation.

  • 1 SDCM — indistinguishable to almost any observer. Rare, expensive, specified for museums and high-end retail.
  • ≤ 3 SDCM — architectural default. Side-by-side differences are only visible to trained eyes.
  • ≤ 5 SDCM — acceptable for most commercial and task lighting; visible on adjacent white walls.
  • > 5 SDCM — not acceptable for architectural linear or wall wash. Reject.

Practical rules

  • Single bin per project: buy enough material from one bin to cover production plus 10–15 % spares, including warranty stock. Do not mix bins on linear runs.
  • Duv control: target Duv between −0.003 and +0.003 relative to the black-body locus to avoid the pink/green tint that SDCM alone doesn't catch.
  • Tunable white: when mixing warm and cool channels, tighten the bin spec on both channels — any drift shows up along the dim curve.
  • Incoming QA: spot-check 3–5 pieces per reel with a handheld spectrometer; reject any reel whose centroid falls outside the purchased bin.
Field rule When a customer reports "some sections look pinker than others" on a wall wash, the fault is almost always mixed bins, not dimming or driver issues. The fix is a bin-matched re-ship, not a drive-current tweak.

Optical Design Overview

Primary, secondary, tertiary

  • Primary optic — the package itself (silicone dome, phosphor layer, reflector cup). Sets the bare-LED emission pattern, usually Lambertian ≈ 120°.
  • Secondary optic — TIR lens, reflector, or refractor bonded in front of the package to shape the beam. Controls beam angle, aim, and intensity distribution (circular, elliptical, batwing, asymmetric wall wash).
  • Tertiary optic — diffusers, louvres, honeycombs, snoots, and trims that the beam passes through after the secondary. Controls glare (UGR), uniformity, source visibility, and texture.

Glare control & UGR

Unified Glare Rating (UGR) from EN 12464-1 is the design target for interior luminaires. Values range roughly from 10 (no glare perception) to 30 (severe glare). The common architectural ceilings:

  • UGR < 16 — technical drawing, precision tasks, fine detail.
  • UGR < 19 — offices, classrooms, retail (most common target).
  • UGR < 22 — industrial, reception, circulation.
  • UGR < 25 — coarse tasks, storage, heavy industry.

  • Output type: constant current for single strings; constant voltage for strips
  • Input range: 120–277 V for NA commercial, 100–305 V for universal
  • Dimming: 0–10 V, DALI-2, TRIAC, or PWM — specify on the order
  • Power factor: > 0.9 at full load (code requirement in most jurisdictions)
  • THD: < 20% for commercial, < 10% for utility rebate eligibility
  • Surge: 4 kV line-line / 6 kV line-ground for outdoor
  • IP rating: match or exceed the fixture's rating
  • Warranty: 5 years minimum; 7 years for outdoor Class 1 products

Outdoor & Wet Location

Rating basics

  • IP65 — dust-tight, protected against water jets. Minimum for most exterior wall and sign lighting.
  • IP66 — dust-tight, powerful water jets. Coastal, facade, parking structures.
  • IP67 — dust-tight, short-term submersion to 1 m. In-grade and step lighting.
  • IP68 — continuous submersion to specified depth. Pool, fountain, landscape water features.
  • IP69K — high-pressure, high-temperature washdown. Food processing, transit wash.

Design requirements

  • Gaskets: EPDM or silicone, compression 15–25 %. Confirm compression with a clay pill test at first article. NBR (buna) is not UV stable — avoid for exterior.
  • Fasteners: SS304 minimum, SS316 for coastal / marine / chlorinated environments.
  • Housings: Al 6063-T5 with Type III hard anodize + powder coat, or ADC12 with chromate + AAMA 2605 powder. Never bare aluminum in marine.
  • Drivers: IP67 minimum with 4 kV/6 kV surge. Mean Well HLG, LPF, ELG families are typical. Include a replaceable Type 4 SPD module for lightning-prone sites.
  • Cable glands: brass nickel-plated or stainless. Sized so cable OD sits in the middle of the gland range — do not oversize.
  • Drain holes: 3–5 mm weep holes at the low point of any housing that cannot be fully sealed. Gore Protective Vents (PolyVent PMF series) for IP66/67 housings to equalize pressure and prevent internal condensation pumping.
  • Photometric orientation: rotate wall washers to flood downward wherever practical; in-grade uplights must tilt ≥ 15° off vertical or include louvred shields to control sky glow.

Dark Sky & BUG rating

IDA (International Dark-Sky Association) "Dark Sky Approved" fixtures emit < 2 % of their output above 90°. BUG rating (Backlight, Uplight, Glare) under IES TM-15 scores a fixture on three axes from 0 to 5; municipalities increasingly require BUG scores of U0 and G2 or better. Specify full cut-off optics and shield any uplight that isn't structurally necessary.

Hazardous Locations

Classification (North American)

Division 1 — hazard present under normal conditions. Division 2 — hazard present only under abnormal conditions.

Group specifies the exact material: Groups A/B/C/D for Class I (acetylene, hydrogen, ethylene, propane), E/F/G for Class II (metal, coal, grain).

Design methods

  • Explosionproof (XP, d): robust enclosure contains any internal ignition without propagating flame. Heavy die-cast aluminum or stainless steel with threaded joints, gasketed glass window, ≥ 12 mm wall thickness.
  • Increased safety (e): no sparking or arcing under normal operation. Sealed connectors, reinforced insulation, temperature-limited surfaces.
  • Intrinsically safe (i): energy in the fixture is kept below what could ignite the hazard. Uses barrier-isolated DC drivers.
  • Encapsulation (m): all electrical components fully potted in a resin that prevents gas or dust ingress. Common for LED modules and small drivers.

Certification & marking

  • UL 844 / CSA C22.2 No. 137 — luminaires for use in hazardous (classified) locations.
  • ATEX (Europe) / IECEx (international) — equivalent directives for Zone 0/1/2 (gas) and Zone 20/21/22 (dust).
  • Temperature code (T-code): T1 (450 °C) through T6 (85 °C). Maximum surface temperature must stay below the auto-ignition temperature of the hazardous material.
Design rule A hazardous-location fixture is never a "standard fixture with a thicker enclosure". Every joint, gasket, gland, lens, and fastener must be part of the certified assembly. Substituting any component voids the listing and exposes the installer to criminal liability.

Emergency & Egress Lighting

Regulatory backbone

  • NFPA 101 Life Safety Code — U.S. requirement for emergency illumination along the means of egress.
  • UL 924 — Emergency Lighting and Power Equipment (U.S. listing standard).
  • CSA C22.2 No. 141 — Emergency lighting equipment (Canadian standard).
  • EN 1838 / EN 60598-2-22 — European emergency lighting performance and product requirements.
  • NBCC Sentence 3.2.7 — Canadian National Building Code egress illumination rules.

Performance targets

  • Minimum path illumination: 1 fc (≈ 10 lux) average along the egress path, 0.1 fc (1 lux) minimum at any point.
  • Runtime: 30 minutes minimum (U.S. NFPA 101); 90 minutes in Canada (CSA), in health care, and in high-rise buildings.
  • Uniformity: maximum-to-minimum ratio ≤ 40 : 1 along the path.
  • Transfer time: illumination must reach full level within 10 seconds of AC loss (per NFPA).
  • Self-test: monthly 30-second function test and annual full-duration test, either self-diagnostic or manually logged.

Design patterns

  • Integral battery pack: UL 924 emergency LED driver built into the fixture, powering a percentage of the LEDs on battery for 90 minutes. Typical for linear and downlights.
  • Central inverter: UL 924 listed building-level inverter powers any compatible fixture from its AC output. Cleanest approach for large projects.
  • Dedicated emergency fixtures: separate luminaires (bug-eyes, exit signs) wired directly to the emergency circuit.
  • Generator-backed circuit: lights stay on from a transfer switch — does not eliminate the need for an immediate 10-second battery bridge.
Battery chemistry LiFePO4 is the current default for integral emergency packs: stable across −20 to +60 °C, 2000+ cycles, no thermal runaway. NiCd is legacy and increasingly hard to source; sealed lead-acid is almost gone. Match the battery temperature rating to the housing ambient — mounting an emergency driver inside a hot ceiling plenum is the #1 cause of premature battery failure.

Horticultural Lighting

The units are different

Plants don't see lumens — they see photons in the 400–700 nm photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) band. Horticultural fixtures are specified in photon flux, not luminous flux.

Design requirements

  • PPE target: ≥ 2.5 µmol/J for a competitive horticultural fixture; best-in-class 3.0+ µmol/J.
  • Spectrum: a white-LED-dominant "full spectrum" blended with supplemental deep red (660 nm) and optional far red (730 nm) is the current commercial default.
  • LEDs: Samsung LM301 / LM301H EVO, Osram Oslon Square Hyper Red, Seoul SunLike, Nichia Hortisolis — all purpose-designed for horticulture.
  • Environment: target IP65 minimum. Growrooms are hot (28–32 °C), humid (60–80 % RH), salt-laden, and wash down regularly. Use corrosion-resistant powder coat and SS316 fasteners.
  • Dimming & control: 0–10 V is still the greenhouse standard; DALI-2 and Ethernet-based controllers are rising in commercial indoor farming.
  • Uniformity: aim for ≥ 0.7 min/avg across the canopy. Poor uniformity creates "hot spots" that burn and "cold spots" that stretch.
  • Safety: at PPFD above 500 µmol/m²/s the fixture emits enough blue light to require ANSI/IES RP-27 photobiological risk labeling.
Never specify a horticultural fixture in lumens or lux. It's the single surest way to fail commissioning. Use a quantum sensor (Apogee MQ-500 or equivalent) for every field verification.

PCB & Part Nomenclature

Generic LED board part number pattern

<FAMILY>-<LEDCOUNT>-<WIDTH>-<PITCH>-<Vf>-<SERIES><PARALLEL>-<DRIVER>-<LED>-<REV>

The house rule: a single, parseable part number that encodes every decision a technician needs to substitute boards in the field. Field delimiters and widths are fixed so any script can split the string cleanly.

  • FAMILY — 3–5 letter code for the product family / profile
  • LEDCOUNT — number of LEDs per module
  • WIDTH — PCB width family in the fixture's width table
  • PITCH — centre-to-centre LED pitch in mm
  • Vf — nominal forward voltage of one series string at Tj ≈ 85 °C
  • SERIES × PARALLEL — wiring topology (e.g. 1S-10P, 6S-2P)
  • DRIVER — intended driver family code (ties the board to its validated driver)
  • LED — LED package (2835, 3030, 3535, COB, etc.)
  • REV — board revision letter, incremented on any electrical or copper change
Rule Never change the electrical topology or LED package without bumping the revision letter. Silent revisions are the single most common cause of field flicker and driver mismatches.
